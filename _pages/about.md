## How do people make judgments and decisions when the information they rely on is noisy and incomplete—and how is this changing in the age of AI?

<div class="bio-content">

  <section class="bio-sec">
    <div class="bio-band">
      <img src="/images/current_institution.jpg" alt="Fuqua School of Business">
    </div>
    <p>
      I am currently a DFG-funded Walter Benjamin Fellow and visiting research scholar in the <a href="https://areas.fuqua.duke.edu/management/index.html" target="_blank">Management and Organizations Area</a> at Duke University's Fuqua School of Business in Durham, NC, USA. Previously, I was a research associate in the <a href="https://uni-tuebingen.de/en/174183" target="_blank">Social Cognition and Decision Sciences</a> group in the Department of Psychology at the University of Tübingen in Germany.
    </p>
    <p>
      I earned my PhD <i>(Dr. rer. nat.)</i> in Psychology in 2023 from the Research Training Group <a href="https://www.uni-mannheim.de/smip/team/alumni/dr-tobias-rebholz/" target="_blank">“Statistical Modeling in Psychology” (SMiP)</a>—a DFG-funded, transregional collaboration of the Universities of Mannheim, Freiburg, Heidelberg, Landau, and Tübingen, Germany. My academic foundation lies in Business Studies and Economics, with a BSc-Major in “Psychoeconomics” in 2017 and an MSc-Major in “Econometrics and Applied Economics” in 2020, both from the University of Konstanz, Germany.
    </p>
    <p>
      I serve as an Associate Editor for the <i><a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/page/journal/10990771/homepage/editorialboard.html" target="_blank">Journal of Behavioral Decision Making</a></i> and <i><a href="https://rr.peercommunityin.org/public/user_public_page?userId=11127" target="_blank">Peer Community In: Registered Reports</a></i>. In my role as a website developer, I am also an Associated Executive Board Member of the <a href="https://eadm.eu/executive-board/" target="_blank">European Association for Decision Making</a> (EADM).
    </p>
  </section>

  <section class="bio-sec">
    <div class="bio-band">
      <img src="/images/bias-brain.png" alt="Bias and brain illustration">
    </div>
    <p>
      Given the name I was dealt, studying what tends “to bias” estimates and behavior feels like nominative determinism—or, at times, an occupational hazard. In other words, my <a href="/research/">research</a> focuses on investigating biases both in a statistical sense (i.e., improving the estimation of effects and phenomena) as well as from a psychological perspective—specifically, in people’s judgment and decision-making (JDM).
    </p>
    <p>
      As an interdisciplinary scholar and open-science enthusiast, I use methods from statistics, econometrics, machine learning, and behavioral science to address questions about human and AI behavior in social cognition and human-computer interaction. For instance, a central theme in my current work is quantifying the influence of qualitative information—especially text generated by large language models (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s ChatGPT or Google’s Gemini—on how humans form judgments, make choices, and update their beliefs. More broadly, I develop and apply advanced statistical and machine learning methods to understand how ecological constraints shape information sampling and utilization in individual, organizational, and societal contexts.
    </p>
    <p>
      My research has secured over 150,000 EUR in competitive funding and has been published in prestigious outlets, including <i>Psychological Inquiry</i>, <i>Judgment and Decision Making</i>, the <i>Journal of Behavioral Decision Making</i>, and the <i>Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied</i>.
    </p>
  </section>
